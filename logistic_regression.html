Section 1

Binary Outcomes in Medicine

Most clinical decisions are binary: treat or don't treat, admit or discharge, malignant or benign. Logistic Regression is the statistical workhorse behind these decisions because it predicts the probability of a binary event.

  • 🦠
    Surgical Site Infection Did the patient develop an infection within 30 days of the operation? (Infected / Not Infected)
  • 🏥
    Hospital Readmission Was the patient readmitted within 30 days of discharge? (Readmitted / Not Readmitted)
  • 🔬
    Tumour Diagnosis Based on biopsy findings, is the tissue malignant or benign? (Malignant / Benign)
💡

Why not Linear Regression? Linear regression can predict values outside [0, 1], producing impossible probabilities like −0.2 or 1.4. Logistic Regression guarantees outputs between 0% and 100%.

✅ Correct! Readmission (yes/no) is a classic binary outcome with exactly two mutually exclusive values — perfect for logistic regression.
❌ Not quite. Readmission is a yes/no event — it has exactly two outcomes, making it binary. Logistic regression is designed for exactly this type of problem.
❓ MCQ 1.2
Why is ordinary linear regression unsuitable for predicting the probability of malignancy from a biopsy?

✅ Exactly! Linear regression can produce values like −0.3 or 1.5 — meaningless as probabilities. Logistic regression solves this by mapping any real number to the (0, 1) interval via the sigmoid function.
❌ The key issue is that linear regression produces values outside [0, 1], giving impossible probabilities. That's why we need the sigmoid transformation of logistic regression.
Section 2

The Decision Boundary

Before we can classify patients, we need a dividing line — a boundary that separates those who are High Risk from those who are Low Risk.

📊

From "What" to "So What"

Features like Age or Blood Pressure tell us what is happening. The decision line tells us what to do — it bridges observation and intervention.

🌫️

The Problem of Overlap

Healthy and sick patients often have similar vitals. Without a decision line, we are stuck in a gray zone where a clear treatment decision is impossible.

🎚️

Simplifying Complexity

A patient may have five different risk factors. The decision boundary collapses all those signals into a single "Yes/No" answer.

❓ MCQ 2.1
In logistic regression, what does a risk score of z = 0 indicate about a patient's position relative to the decision boundary?
The patient is definitely in the high-risk category
The patient is exactly on the boundary (50/50 probability)
The patient has no measurable risk factors
The model failed to converge for that patient
✅ Correct! When z = 0, the sigmoid function returns exactly 0.5 (50%). The patient is sitting precisely on the decision boundary — neither clearly in the low-risk nor high-risk zone.
❌ When z = 0, sigmoid(0) = 0.5 — a 50/50 probability. The patient is exactly on the decision boundary, in a neutral position.
❓ MCQ 2.2
A patient's risk score increases from z = 1 to z = 3. What is the most accurate interpretation of this change?
The patient has moved further into the high-risk zone, with a higher predicted probability of the adverse outcome
The patient's probability of the adverse outcome tripled
The patient crossed the decision boundary from low-risk to high-risk
The change in z represents the exact change in probability
✅ Correct! Both z = 1 and z = 3 are already in the high-risk zone (z > 0), but z = 3 implies a higher probability via the sigmoid function. The relationship is non-linear — z scores don't map directly to probability differences.
❌ The patient was already in the high-risk zone at z = 1. Moving to z = 3 increases probability further, but not proportionally — the sigmoid function creates a non-linear mapping between z and probability.
Section 3

The Sigmoid Transformation

Clinicians think in probabilities, not raw scores. The sigmoid function squashes any real-valued score (z) into a meaningful probability between 0 and 1.

p(X) = 1 / (1 + e−z) Where z = β₀ + β₁X₁ + β₂X₂ + … is the linear risk score

❓ MCQ 3.1
A logistic regression model produces a raw score of z = −2.5 for a patient. What does the sigmoid function output?
A probability of exactly 2.5%
A probability greater than 50%
A negative probability, indicating "definite absence"
A probability less than 50%, since z is negative
✅ Correct! Sigmoid(−2.5) ≈ 0.076, i.e. about 7.6%. Any negative z value maps to a probability below 50%, indicating lower risk. The sigmoid always outputs between 0 and 1.
❌ When z is negative, sigmoid(z) < 0.5. Sigmoid(−2.5) ≈ 7.6% — a low-probability, low-risk classification. Probabilities are always between 0 and 1.
❓ MCQ 3.2
Why does logistic regression use the sigmoid function rather than directly outputting the linear score z?

To constrain outputs to [0, 1] so they can be interpreted as probabilities
To ensure the model can handle more than two classes

✅ Right! The sigmoid "squashes" unbounded z values (−∞ to +∞) into the (0, 1) range. This transforms meaningless scores into interpretable probabilities that clinicians can act on.
❌ The purpose of the sigmoid is to map any real-valued score into the [0, 1] range, producing interpretable probabilities rather than unbounded numbers.
Section 4

Interpreting Coefficients — The Odds Ratio

The raw β coefficients are hard to interpret. By exponentiating them (eβ), we obtain Odds Ratios — a concept deeply familiar to clinical researchers.

Odds Ratio = eβ An OR > 1 means the predictor increases risk · OR < 1 means it decreases risk
Predictor Coefficient (β) Odds Ratio (eᵝ) Clinical Interpretation
Age (per year) 0.04 1.04 Each additional year of age increases the odds by ~4%
Smoking (yes vs no) 0.69 2.00 Smokers have twice the odds of the adverse outcome
BMI (per unit) 0.05 1.05 Each BMI unit adds ~5% to the odds
Treatment A (yes vs no) −0.51 0.60 Treatment reduces the odds by ~40%

❓ MCQ 4.1
A logistic regression model predicts 30-day mortality. The coefficient for "Diabetes" is β = 0.916. What is the Odds Ratio and its interpretation?

OR = 0.4; diabetes is associated with a protective effect
OR ≈ 2.5; diabetic patients have about 2.5× the odds of mortality
OR ≈ 0.916; the risk is 91.6% for diabetic patients
✅ Correct! e^0.916 ≈ 2.5. Diabetic patients have approximately 2.5 times the odds of 30-day mortality compared to non-diabetic patients, holding other factors constant.
❌ The Odds Ratio = e^β = e^0.916 ≈ 2.5. A positive β means increased risk. Diabetic patients have about 2.5 times the odds of the outcome.

❌ e^(−0.693) ≈ 0.5. A negative β produces an OR < 1, indicating a protective association. Statin users have approximately 50% lower odds of ICU admission.
Section 5

How the Model Learns — MLE & Log Loss

Logistic regression doesn't use Ordinary Least Squares. Instead, it finds weights that maximize the likelihood of observing the actual patient outcomes — a process called Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE).

🎯

Maximum Likelihood Estimation

Find the β weights that make the observed clinical data most probable. If a patient had a complication and the model predicted 95% — great. If it predicted 5% — penalise heavily.

⚡

Log Loss (Cross-Entropy)

The "punishment" system. Predicting 0.05 for a patient who actually had the event receives a near-infinite penalty. Predicting 0.95 for the same patient yields a near-zero penalty.

⛰️

Gradient Descent

The algorithm iteratively adjusts weights in the direction that reduces the Log Loss — like rolling a ball down a hill until it reaches the lowest point.

❓ MCQ 5.1
A logistic regression model predicts a 0.02 probability of mortality for a patient who actually died. Which statement about the log loss penalty is correct?
The penalty is very small because the probability is close to zero
The penalty is exactly 0.02 units
The penalty is very large — log(0.02) creates a severe punishment
No penalty is applied because the model tried its best
✅ Exactly! Log loss for y=1 is −log(predicted probability). −log(0.02) ≈ 3.91, a very large penalty. When the model is confident in the wrong direction, the loss grows dramatically.
❌ Log loss = −log(p) for a positive case. −log(0.02) ≈ 3.91 — an enormous penalty. This is by design: confidently wrong predictions are punished severely to push the model toward better calibration.
❓ MCQ 5.2
What is the goal of Gradient Descent in logistic regression training?
Iteratively adjust the model weights to minimise the total log loss across all training patients
Find the decision boundary by sorting patients by risk score
Maximise the number of correct predictions regardless of confidence
Randomly sample subsets of patients to improve generalisation
✅ Correct! Gradient descent iteratively nudges the β weights in the direction that reduces log loss — like walking downhill on the loss surface until convergence at the optimal weights.
❌ Gradient descent is an optimisation algorithm that iteratively adjusts β weights to reduce the total log loss. It follows the gradient (slope) of the loss surface downward until reaching a minimum.
Section 6

Why Logistic Regression Thrives in Clinical Settings

Despite being decades old, logistic regression remains the gold standard for clinical prediction models — including APACHE, SOFA, and FIB-4.

🗣️

It Speaks "Risk"

Odds Ratios align with how clinicians already think. "Smoking doubles your risk" is far more actionable than "the neural network increased the activation by 0.3".

📦

Bounded Outputs

Probabilities always land between 0% and 100%. The model will never say "120% chance of survival" — a guarantee linear regression cannot make.

🔍

The Glass Box

Unlike deep learning, every coefficient is inspectable. A physician can trace exactly which factors drove the risk estimate and justify it to patients or ethics committees.

🧑‍🔬

Small Sample Friendly

Works well with dozens of patients. Deep learning usually needs thousands of training examples to perform well.

⚠️ Model Limitations

Linear Decision Boundary: Logistic regression draws a straight line. If the true boundary between classes is curved, the model will underperform — consider tree-based methods for non-linear relationships.

Multicollinearity: When two predictors are highly correlated (e.g., BMI and Weight), the model struggles to assign reliable coefficients to each. Always check your VIF before reporting results.

❓ MCQ 6.1
A hospital committee asks why the AI model flagged a patient as "High Risk for sepsis". Which property of logistic regression makes this easiest to answer?
Logistic regression is faster to run than deep learning
It requires fewer training patients than other models
It produces perfectly accurate predictions
Its coefficients (odds ratios) reveal exactly which factors drove the classification
✅ This is the "Glass Box" advantage. The clinician can present each predictor's odds ratio to explain the decision: "The patient's age (OR 1.6) and elevated CRP (OR 2.3) were the primary drivers of the high-risk flag."
❌ The "Glass Box" property is key here. Each coefficient has an interpretable Odds Ratio, so clinicians can explain exactly which risk factors drove the model's decision — unlike a deep learning "Black Box".
❓ MCQ 6.2
A researcher finds that two predictors — "Total Cholesterol" and "LDL Cholesterol" — have a correlation of r = 0.95 in their dataset. Which logistic regression limitation does this trigger?
Linear assumption violation — the data is not linearly separable
Multicollinearity — highly correlated predictors make coefficients unstable and unreliable
Overfitting — too many predictors for the sample size
Class imbalance — the outcome events are too rare
✅ Correct! Multicollinearity occurs when predictors are highly correlated. The model cannot reliably separate their individual contributions, leading to inflated standard errors and unstable Odds Ratios. Removing or combining correlated predictors is the standard remedy.
❌ r = 0.95 is severe multicollinearity. When predictors overlap almost completely, the model can't isolate their individual effects — coefficients become unstable and standard errors balloon. One of the predictors should typically be removed.
